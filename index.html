How a Single Storm Broke Sri Lanka

A data story exploring how Cyclone Ditwah paralyzed our infrastructure, stopped school for 526,609 children, and left a critical gap in the recovery effort.

By Chatura Dissanayake & Imali Nillekumbura | Published March 26, 2026 | 8 Min Read

On November 28, 2025, life as we knew it stopped. Cyclone Ditwah hit our eastern shores with a force not felt in a generation. Within four hours of landfall, gale-force winds of 120km/h plunged nearly 4 million people into total darkness as standard grid systems simultaneously collapsed.

Early reports from the ground were fragmented, painting a grim but incomplete picture of the coastal damage. It would take months for the floodwaters to fully recede and the isolated highland communities to be reached. Only then did the true, compounding scale of the disaster reveal itself.

The sheer volume of rainfall saturated the terraced slopes beyond their critical threshold, triggering geological collapse.

The Anatomy of the Storm

Cyclone Ditwah did not strike uniformly; it dragged its destruction across the island in three distinct, devastating phases. While the south faced immediate coastal inundation, the center experienced geological collapse, and the north endured the long-term ruin of its agricultural basins. Each phase required a different emergency response, splintering the already stretched national resources.

← Swipe maps to explore phases →
Phase 01

The Landfall

Cyclone Ditwah initiated its assault on the southern coastlines. It brought an unprecedented storm surge that instantly overwhelmed coastal defenses, submerging primary highways within an hour.

Phase 02

Highland Collapse

The system moved inland and stalled over the central hills, dumping a month's worth of rain in roughly 12 hours. The saturated earth triggered hundreds of landslides, isolating entire towns.

Phase 03

The Northern Exit

Finally, the weakened storm system tracked northwards. While wind speeds dropped, it left a trail of severe flash flooding across the northern plains, devastating the primary agricultural zones just before harvest.

The Silent Handoff

As the immediate physical danger subsided, a slower, more insidious crisis began: human displacement. Tracking the location of the displaced reveals a crucial "crossover" moment in late December. As official state-run safety centers closed, the people inside them did not go home—they couldn't. Instead, they were quietly absorbed into the homes of neighbors, extended family, and complete strangers.

The Unresolved Displacement Arc

By January, the state stepped back and host families absorbed the burden silently. The crisis did not end in December; new rains in March forced over 149,000 people back into emergency centers.

Survival Over Schooling

The displacement crisis extended far beyond broken buildings into the institutions designed to protect the future. While 1,339 schools were severely damaged structurally, the intact schools were immediately requisitioned and repurposed into emergency safety hubs. This created a zero-sum game: you cannot resume classes if the classroom is actively being used as a bedroom for displaced families.

The Chain Reaction

When the floodwaters finally receded, they left a silent, long-term economic crisis buried in the soil. Nearly 20% of the country's cultivated land—over 148,410 hectares—was wiped out in a single weekend. The foundation of the nation's food supply fractured overnight, leaving 70,000 smallholder farmers without insurance. This physical damage triggered an immediate chain reaction, resulting in a devastating surge in basic market pricing for urban populations.

The Cost of Silence

Physical and economic recovery requires significant, immediate capital. The United Nations launched the Humanitarian Priorities Plan (HPP) to coordinate this, yet international aid has been severely fragmented. Major donors opted to bypass the coordinated system entirely, creating a "shadow flow" of funds that failed to reach the most critical targets while the official response ran dry.

The Scars Left Behind

The story of Cyclone Ditwah isn't over—it is simply getting quieter. With agricultural funding still facing a massive deficit and over 149,000 people yet to return home, the recovery rate has completely stalled. We cannot just wait for the next monsoon. Bridging this gap is the only way to ensure our island stays resilient against the storms to come.
